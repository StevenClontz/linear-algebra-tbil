Item 2.

We need to show either that \(S\) fails to preserve either vector addition or that \(S\) fails to preserve scalar multiplication.
Let’s compare \(2S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right)\) and \(S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\right)\text{.}\) If \(S\) were linear, then we would expect these to be equal. We compute:
\begin{equation*} 2S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right) = 2\left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ -3 \end{array} \right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}6 \\ -6 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
On the other hand, we find that:
\begin{equation*} S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\right)=\left[\begin{array}{c}6 \\ -12 \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}
Since these expressions are unequal, we have found an example demonstrating that \(S\) does not preserve scalar multiplication in general and is therefore not a linear map.
Alternatively, we could attempt to find an example that demonstrates that \(S\) does not preserve vector addition. Let’s compare \(S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right)\) and \(S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]\right)+S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right)\text{.}\) If \(S\) were linear, then we would expect these to be equal. We compute:
\begin{equation*} S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ -3 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
On the other hand, we find that:
\begin{equation*} S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]\right)+S\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\right)=\left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\0 \end{array} \right]+\left[\begin{array}{c}2 \\0 \end{array} \right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\0 \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}
Since these expressions are unequal, we have found an example demonstrating that \(S\) does not preserve vector addition in general and is therefore not a linear map.
